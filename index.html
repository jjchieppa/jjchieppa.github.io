My Research

Southern Pine Decline and Ecosystem Vulnerability

I did my MSc in the Forest Health Dynamics Laboratory at Auburn University's Schhol of Forestry and Wildlife Sciences. There, I worked on Southern Pine Decline (SPD), a disease complex affecting productivity of southern pine species, including loblolly pine. Specifically, stressed pine trees (predisopsing edaphic, climatic, or interactive genetic factors) cause trees to release volatile compounds, which attracts bark beetles. In the case of SPD, beetles are root feeding and vector pathogenic fungi. Altogether, mature trees exhibiting symptoms of SPD show crown dieback, reduced productivity, a loss of fine root biomass.

For my thesis, I worked with seedlings of loblolly pine. We inoculated seedlings with fungi associated with SPD. Genotypes (some resistant, some tolerant to SPD fungi) we grown in open-top chambers (OTCs) at the Atmospheric Deposition Research Facility. Following exposure to elevated tropospheric ozone (first experiment) or fluctuating moisture supply (drought, second experiment), we assessed the interactive effects of climatic stressors with SPD fungi, and potential genetic relationships for cross tolerance.

While working on southern pine decline research, I took on a side project to explore urban heat islands (UHIs) in small cities. Using a machine learning-based classification approach, we applied the Local Climate Zone (LCZ) system to satellite imagery, identifying urban and rural zones in Auburn and Opelika, Alabama. By deploying temperature sensors in multiple LCZs, we assessed how urban development influenced local temperatures. Results confirmed urban areas were consistently warmer, but UHI intensity varied even in similar zones, suggesting that LCZ-based classifications alone may not fully predict heat retention. This study demonstrated the potential of ML-driven remote sensing for urban climate analysis while highlighting its limitations in smaller, heterogeneous landscapes.
