Southern Pine Decline and Environmental Stressors

Southern pine decline is a complex syndrome affecting loblolly pine (*Pinus taeda*) and other southeastern U.S. pine species, often linked to environmental stressors and root-infecting ophiostomatoid fungi. My research explores how two key factors—elevated tropospheric ozone and variable summer precipitation—interact with fungal susceptibility in loblolly pine seedlings. Using controlled greenhouse experiments, I assessed physiological responses, fungal colonization, and host susceptibility under simulated ozone exposure and shifting rainfall patterns.

Findings suggest that environmental stressors can exacerbate mechanical damage in seedlings, potentially influencing disease progression, though direct fungal colonization patterns remain variable. Genetic variation within loblolly pine families plays a critical role in determining resilience, with some families exhibiting greater susceptibility to both fungal infection and environmental extremes. This work provides insight into the physiological trade-offs between abiotic stress tolerance and biotic resistance, contributing to a broader understanding of southern pine decline.

By integrating plant physiology, pathology, and climate stressors, my research highlights the need for a holistic approach to forest management. As climate change alters atmospheric conditions and precipitation patterns, understanding these interactions will be key to predicting and mitigating the future impacts of disease on pine forests in the southeastern U.S.
